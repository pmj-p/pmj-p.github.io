Good evening, dear experts/ˈekspɜːrts/ and scholars. My name is Pan Mengjie from Zhejiang University of Technology. It’s my great honor to share with my paper, which was accepted/əkˈseptɪd/ at IJCNN. The title is “IAFI-FCOS: Intra- and Across-layer Feature Interaction FCOS Model for Lesion Detection in CT Images.” The main work of this paper is to design a lesion detector to detect the lesions in medical CT images. Next, I will introduce it from the following five aspects/ˈæspekts/.

The first part is the research background and motivation. Cancer is a major global public health problem, and the graph visualizes new cancer incidence and mortality by 2024(twenty twenty four), showing that nearly half of the world’s new cancer cases and deaths occur in Asia. In Africa and some Asian regions, although the incidence of cancer is not very high, cancer mortality is high, in part because many patients are diagnosed
/ˌdaɪəɡˈnoʊst/ with cancer at an advanced stage, resulting in a higher mortality rate. If you can in the occurrence of lesions, timely treatment will improve the survival/sərˈvaɪv(ə)l/ rate.

When a patient is unwell, doctors usually use medical images to diagnose, for example, lesions in CT images may deteriorate/dɪˈtɪriəreɪt/ into cancer. However, in the early stage of cancer, the characteristics/ˌkærɪktəˈrɪstɪks/ of the lesions are not obvious, and doctors often rely on their past experience when screening. At the same time, when screening a large number of CT images, it will consume time and energy, which will lead to misdiagnosis and missed detection. The use of computer-aided detection can help doctors automatically identify the location of suspicious/səˈspɪʃəs/ lesions and reduce their workload. CADe is the use of object detection technology to detect the lesion area. Object detection is mainly studied on natural images and is generally divided into three categories: two-stage, one-stage, and transformer structure. However, the direct application of these algorithms to the field of medical imaging does not guarantee/ˌɡærənˈtiː/ satisfactory performance, because the differences between medical images and natural images can lead to the final lesion detection results being less accurate.

We have compiled papers related to computer-aided detection and summarized that the current lesion detection task faces several main challenges: firstly, lesion detection not only relies on features specific to the lesion region, but also requires the assistance of surrounding tissue information. For example, in the pancreatic tumor data set, mucous cystic tumors are generally located in the pancreatic body and tail, and the introduction of this location information can better assist the identification of lesions. Second, the traditional object detector has two shortcomings in multi-scale feature fusion. One is that direct fuse of different layers of features reduces the representation of multi-scale features, and the other is that top-down transfer of features leads to loss of information. When features are fused, if the feature information of hard-to-detect lesions is lost, the overall accuracy/ˈækjərəsi/ of the model will be reduced. These problems can cause small lesions and borderless lesions to be difficult to identify normally.

To address the above problems, we propose a lesion detection based on a one stage detector. The main contribution of this paper is that we propose intra-layer context augmentation block and cross-layer feature weighting block to solve these two problems respectively. The proposed IAFI-FCOS model obtained good results on both pancreas dataset and deeplesion dataset.

The second part is the methodology/ˌmeθəˈdɑːlədʒi/. First, as shown in the figure, is a traditional detection framework, which is mainly composed of backbone, neck and head. The backbone is used to extract multi-scale features, the neck fuses multi-scale feature information, and head outputs overall prediction results, such as detection boxes and categories. The Neck part plays an important role in the detection model. The traditional neck generally uses the structure of FPN, which sums up different layers of features from top to bottom. This structure does not consider the two challenges mentioned above. Therefore, we proposed intra-layer context augmentation block to alleviate/əˈliːvieɪt/ Challenge 1, and cross-layer feature weighting block to reduce the loss of detail information.

This figure shows our overall network structure, compared with the traditional detection framework, we mainly adjusted and optimized for the neck part. The traditional FPN structure is replaced by using our proposed ICAF-FPN. The ICAF-FPN is mainly divided into three parts: ICA Block, AFW Block, and AFF. Step one: After the backbone network outputs the multi–scale features, the feature maps of each layer are processed by the ICA Block to obtain the intra-layer feature, respectively. The ICA block mainly consists of the Multi-head dilated/daɪˈleɪtɪd/ Attention and MLP. Step 2: The multi-layer feature maps are input into AFW Block together and layer 2 is introduced because layer 2 retains more small target feature information. the AFW Block consists of the across-layer feature gather (AFG) block and the split feature weighting (Split FW ) block, which outputs the inter-layer fusion feature; Step 3: we use the residual/rɪˈzɪdʒuəl/ structure to supplement/ˈsʌplɪmənt/ the feature information to get Li. Finally: the adaptive feature fusion mechanism is used to fuse these three different semantic features. Above is the specific flow of our method.

I will expand on the details of the ICA Block and AFW Block. The ICA Block sets different dilation rates for different heads of multi-head attention, which captures the context information of different receptive fields and establishes/ɪˈstæblɪʃɪz/ the dependency between pixels. Then AFW Block aligns the multilayer features using the AFG Block and extracts global features using dual-axis attention. The global features are weighted to different layers using Split FW Block and finally aligned features are fused. The details of the method can be followed up by reading our paper.

Then, the third section briefly/ˈbriːfli/ describes the experiments and configurations. We performed experimental analysis on two datasets, a private pancreas dataset and a public deeplesion dataset. All data are pre-processed according to expert recommendations. The evaluation matrices
/ˈmeɪtrɪsiːz/ of the experiments mainly use the Average Precision (AP) metric and the Free-Response Receiver Operating Characteristic (FROC), and the specific application details are described in the paper and PPT accordingly.

Part four: Result and Analysis. Table one shows the comparison experiments on the pancreas dataset, and compared with other object detectors, our method is more competitive. Compared to the baseline, the AP improves by six percent, and for small target recognition also improved by about seven percent, which is a very large improvement. Also comparing to a more stringent metric like FROC, our sensitivity is also improved.

Table 2 and Figure 5 show the results on the deeplesion dataset. Compared with the pancreas dataset, the deeplesion dataset has more diverse/daɪˈvɜːrs/ lesion types and the proportion/prəˈpɔːrʃ(ə)n/ of small targets is small, which makes detection more difficult. Our method also improves the accuracy of lesion detection.

Figure 6 visualizes the detection results of the baseline and our method, (a) is the results of the baseline and (b) is our method. It can be seen that there are no false and missed detections in our method, while there are errors in the baseline. In order to verify/ˈverɪfaɪ/ the effectiveness of our proposed FPN, the results of other different FPNs were compared, and the results are shown in Table 3. Finally, to summarize our work, we mainly propose a lesion detector to assist radiologists in identifying lesions faster and more accurately. This detector mainly optimized the feature fusion to alleviate/əˈliːvieɪt/ the problems existing in computer-aided detection and improve the accuracy of detection. The large number of experiments in the paper have proved the effectiveness of our method, but we still need more efforts to realize the generalization in various disease/dɪˈziːz/ detection fields. In our future research, we plan to optimize the model according to the characteristics of medical image data. For example, CT images is generally three dimensional/daɪˈmenʃən(ə)l/ structure, at present, we have only optimized the model from the two-dimensional level, and the follow-up work can combine the relationship between different slices to optimize our method from the three dimensional perspective/pərˈspektɪv/.
